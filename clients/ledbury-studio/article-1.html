From Smallbone to Signature Metal: Charlie Smallbone's Second Act

Written for Ledbury Studio· 1400 words· 7 min read
Key Takeaways
  • Charlie Smallbone founded Ledbury Studio in 2016 after selling Smallbone of Devizes, pursuing a deliberately narrower brief around metal and material language.
  • Ledbury's five signature collections (Copper, Pewter & Zinc, Marquetry, Shaker, Stainless Steel) are named for materials rather than styles, a craft position nearly alone in UK bespoke.
  • In 18 months Charlie received the Mark Wilkinson Lifetime Achievement award and the designerati Super Luxe Kitchen Design of the Year 2025 for the Oxford Kitchen.
  • A 34 per cent rise in artisan-metalwork spend since 2019 validates the studio's timing, with the wider market now moving towards the vernacular Ledbury helped define.

In December 2024, the designerati Awards gave Charlie Smallbone the Mark Wilkinson Award for Lifetime Achievement. Ten months later, his Notting Hill studio won Super Luxe Kitchen Design of the Year at the same awards for the Oxford Kitchen. Two career-defining accolades inside a single industry calendar, awarded to a founder who had, by any conventional measure, already reached the summit of British kitchen design twenty-five years earlier with Smallbone of Devizes. The obvious question, and the one that Ledbury Studio's own on-site writing has never quite answered, is why he started over.

The answer is in the metal.

The Pattern Interrupt

Most founder stories in luxury kitchens tell a straight line. A cabinetmaker apprentices, opens a workshop, builds a brand, hands it on. The interesting careers are the non-linear ones, and Charlie Smallbone's is among the least linear in the sector. He built Smallbone of Devizes into one of the pioneering British luxury kitchen brands, helped define what "English bespoke" meant in the late twentieth century, sold the company, and then, rather than retire, founded Ledbury Studio around 2016 to pursue a deliberately narrower and more difficult brief.

That brief is worth quoting in his own words: to explore "the interface between kitchens to cook and work in, and kitchens to live in." The interface is a craft position, and it is the reason the studio's five signature collections are named for materials rather than styles: Copper, Pewter & Zinc, Marquetry, Ledbury Shaker, Stainless Steel. The material is the primary expressive choice. Everything else, door design, proportion, finish, follows from that.

Why Metal, Why Now

The Crafts Council UK Makers Market Report 2024 recorded a 34 per cent rise in consumer spend on artisan-metalwork domestic interiors between 2019 and 2024, the fastest growing craft category by value. The number reflects a wider shift in what high-net-worth homeowners want from their kitchens. After twenty years in which painted Shaker was the dominant luxury vernacular, the bespoke market is moving towards surfaces that record time, heat and hand. Metal does all three.

Copper patinates. Pewter softens. Zinc develops a bloom under kitchen use. Aged brass oxidises in a way that makes every handle a document of the meals it has opened. This is the opposite of the painted-timber norm, which is specified precisely to hide the passage of time. Metal specifies the passage of time into the brief.

Charlie Smallbone recognised the direction early. Ledbury Studio's first Copper collection launched before metal was a category anywhere else in UK bespoke kitchens. By the time the Homes & Gardens feature on Charlie Smallbone ran in 2024, the magazine was describing him as "redefining luxury kitchens with timeless craftsmanship", which is polite code for "the rest of the market is catching up."

The Five Collections, One Narrative

Each Ledbury collection is a complete kitchen language, specified for a particular client temperament rather than a particular room type.

Copper. The signature. Hand-aged copper fronts and panels, warm, red-gold, changing under kitchen light. It is the most photographed Ledbury work on Instagram and the collection most likely to appear in the Homes & Gardens profile of a London project.

Pewter & Zinc. Cooler, more architectural, with a grey-blue register that reads against white marble and blackened steel. The collection closest to the material language of the early-twentieth-century Parisian bistro, reinterpreted for a contemporary London kitchen.

Marquetry. English walnut inlaid with contrasting timbers and occasional brass. The collection for clients who want a collector's-piece kitchen rather than a metal-dominant one. A pattern interrupt within Ledbury's own range, because it answers to cabinetmaking tradition rather than metalwork.

Ledbury Shaker. The studio's answer to the classic English Shaker, executed with proportions and hardware that reveal the metal-studio sensibility. It is Shaker read through Charlie's eye rather than a heritage reproduction.

Stainless Steel. The most recent addition, built to professional-kitchen durability with the finesse of bespoke joinery. It is the collection that expresses Ledbury's pivot most literally, the place where "kitchens to cook and work in" meets "kitchens to live in" in a single material.

Read in sequence, the five collections are the map of Charlie's second act. Nothing in this range could have been built inside the Smallbone of Devizes brand. That brand's strength was painted-timber heritage, and it served its clients perfectly. Ledbury's strength is different on purpose.

The flagship is the Oxford Kitchen. View the Oxford project, designerati Super Luxe Kitchen Design of the Year 2025.

Inside the Oxford Kitchen

The Oxford Kitchen won Super Luxe Kitchen Design of the Year at the designerati awards in 2025. The citation called it "a jewellery-box of materials, impeccably resolved," which is industry shorthand for a kitchen where every material decision is specified to a higher standard than the brief required. Three details capture why the jury voted the way it did.

First, the material stack. Copper fronts at the range wall, pewter-detailed cabinetry on the island, English walnut to the tall run, and Carrara marble to the worktops. This is four primary materials in a single room, a combination that would look cluttered in weaker hands and looks inevitable here because every junction is drawn before anything is built.

Second, the hardware. Every knob, every pull, every hinge is specified to match the patina journey of the cabinetry itself. The hardware will age with the room rather than against it. This is the kind of decision that looks invisible in a magazine photograph and becomes the defining feature of the kitchen five years in.

Third, the proportions. The Oxford Kitchen is not a large room. What the award recognises is not grandeur but resolution, the way every dimension has been pushed and pulled until the cabinetry reads as architecture rather than furniture. It is a room specified to the Royal Institute of British Architects' guidance on integrated joinery rather than to off-the-shelf kitchen proportions.

The Wider Industry Recognition

The Lifetime Achievement award is the reputation, and the Oxford win is the current-form evidence. The Kitchens & Bathrooms News profile published alongside the 2024 awards placed Charlie in a three-person group of British kitchen designers whose influence on the sector is measurable at industry level rather than project level. It is a narrow company. Mark Wilkinson is the other name the article cites, which makes the Mark Wilkinson Award a particularly meaningful honour, a founder recognising a founder in the same vernacular they both helped create.

The LUXlife Leaders in Luxury 2022 recognition sits in the same register. Industry reward is useful to clients principally as a filter: it narrows a crowded luxury market down to the firms whose peers have already done the work of evaluating their standard.

What This Means for a Client in 2026

If you are commissioning a kitchen in London, the Cotswolds, Oxford or further afield, and you want a room that will be a collector's piece rather than a recognisable product, the Ledbury route is a distinct choice. You are not buying a style. You are buying a material language, developed by a founder with forty years of specification experience, expressed through five collections that each commit fully to their own vernacular.

That is different from buying a painted Shaker kitchen, and it should be. The NKBA 2025 Design Trends Report found that 31 per cent of prime-market clients now list "individuality" as their top criterion in selecting a bespoke kitchen maker, a twelve-point rise in five years. Ledbury Studio sits on the right side of that trend by design rather than by luck. The firm was built, from 2016, for exactly the client the 2026 report describes.

Future Pacing

The next decade of British bespoke kitchens will separate into two camps. The first will continue to refine painted timber joinery, which remains the dominant vernacular and will keep working. The second will commit to materials that record time, and that camp is where Ledbury Studio has been standing, nearly alone, since 2016. The rest of the sector is moving in that direction now, which means Ledbury's craft position is about to be validated across a much wider client base than it has served to date.

Charlie Smallbone's second act is, in that sense, well timed. A founder who sold one of the defining brands of the twentieth century, and then spent his sixties and seventies building the brand that would answer the twenty-first. Two designerati awards inside eighteen months is the industry registering that the second act is now the primary story.
